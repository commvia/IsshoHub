Chapter 5 — Intersections & Pedestrian Protection

Intersections are where most accidents happen, which is why the written exam tests priority rules in fine detail. This chapter covers two areas: right-of-way at intersections, and the obligation to protect pedestrians, children, the elderly, and persons with disabilities.

5-1　Signalled Intersections

You may proceed on green, but you must still observe the priority order below. Many people assume "green light = I have absolute priority" — this is wrong. Even on green, there are road users you must yield to.

🚦 Yield Order on Green

Your Direction

Who You Must Yield To

Going straight

・Pedestrians currently crossing the road

・"残存歩行者" (zanzon hokōsha) — pedestrians still mid-crossing when the green pedestrian signal begins to flash

Turning left

・Pedestrians and cyclists crossing the crosswalk on your left (the most commonly missed)

・Two-wheeled vehicles going straight from behind you (you cannot cut in front of them)

Turning right

・Oncoming vehicles going straight — including motorcycles (motorcycles are easily overlooked by car drivers)

・Oncoming vehicles turning left

・Pedestrians crossing the crosswalk on your right

💡 Key Concept

In law, "having priority" does not mean "I can go first, full stop" — it means "the other party has a duty to yield to me." Even if you have right-of-way, you must still avoid danger if you see it. Priority does not equal immunity from liability.

5-2　Unsignalled Intersections

At intersections with no signals and no police officer, determine priority using the rules below.

📋 Priority Checklist (work through from top to bottom)

Step

Situation

What to Do

1

The other road has a "一時停止" (stop) or "徐行" (slow) sign

The vehicle facing the sign must stop or slow and yield to the vehicle on the road without the sign.

2

The other vehicle is on a "priority road" (優先道路)

A vehicle entering from a side road must yield to vehicles already on the priority road.

3

The other vehicle is on a clearly wider road

A vehicle entering from a narrower road must yield to vehicles on the wider road.

4

Both roads are the same width, and the other vehicle is coming from the left

The vehicle coming from the left has priority (left-side vehicle priority rule).

5

You want to turn right; the other vehicle is going straight or turning left

The right-turning vehicle must yield to vehicles going straight or turning left.

🧠 One-Line Summary

"Sign → Priority road → Wider road → Vehicle from the left → Straight traffic first" — work through this list in order.

👀 Intersections with Poor Visibility

If your view at an intersection is blocked (e.g. by a large billboard or parked vehicles), you must slow down and creep forward gradually — even if you have right-of-way. Look as you go. This is called 徐行 (jokō, creeping forward).

5-3　Pedestrian Protection Obligations

Japanese traffic law imposes very strict duties to protect pedestrians. In the following situations, vehicles are legally obliged to yield.

Situation

Vehicle's Obligation

A pedestrian is crossing (or about to cross) a crosswalk

✋ Must stop before the crosswalk and yield.

No pedestrian is visible near the crosswalk, but visibility is poor

🐢 Must slow to a speed at which you can stop at any moment (徐行).

A pedestrian is standing near the crosswalk and appears to be about to cross

✋ Must stop and yield — you cannot assume they will wait.

A 残存歩行者 is still mid-crossing when the pedestrian signal starts flashing green

✋ Wait for them to finish crossing before moving, even if your own green light has turned on.

A pedestrian is crossing a road with no marked crosswalk

🐢 Slow down and give way — you must not approach aggressively or use the horn to intimidate.

5-4　Specially Protected Road Users

For the following categories of vulnerable road users, vehicles have a duty to 徐行 (creep) or 一時停止 (come to a full stop and yield) — stricter than the general pedestrian protection rules.

Category

How to Identify

Vehicle's Obligation

Children

Appears to be primary-school age or younger (especially when walking alone)

Slow down and be ready to stop at any moment. Note that children's behaviour is unpredictable (sudden dashes into the road).

Elderly persons

Using a walking stick, moving slowly, visibly elderly

Yield and slow down. Do not hurry them or use the horn.

Visually impaired persons

Carrying a white or yellow cane, wearing dark glasses, accompanied by a guide dog

✋ Must come to a full stop (一時停止) or creep forward (徐行) and yield.

Deaf drivers

Vehicle displays the 蝶々マーク (butterfly mark)

Yield and avoid any intimidating behaviour.

Persons with disabilities

Displaying a disability marker, in a wheelchair, using a prosthetic limb, etc.

✋ Must come to a full stop (一時停止) or creep forward (徐行) and yield.

Nursery / kindergarten groups in transit

An adult accompanying a group of young children on foot

Slow down and pass carefully; be ready to stop at any time.

5-5　School Routes (通学路, tsūgakuro)

A 通学路 is a designated route used by children walking to and from school, typically marked with a 通学路 sign. On these stretches:

・During school commuting hours, reduce your speed and be prepared for children to appear suddenly.

・On some sections, vehicles are prohibited during school commuting hours (look for supplementary time-restriction signs).

・When you see a "飛び出し注意" (watch for children darting out) warning, reduce speed before passing.

5-6　Common Exam Trap Questions

Question

Answer

Explanation

On green, I am turning left and have right-of-way, so I can pass directly over the crosswalk.

✗

A left-turning vehicle must yield to pedestrians crossing the crosswalk.

At an unsignalled intersection with good visibility, vehicles may maintain speed and pass through.

✗

Good visibility allows you to apply the priority rules, but you must still be cautious — "good visibility" does not mean "no need to slow down."

You see a person by the roadside carrying a white cane. Your vehicle must stop or creep forward and yield.

○

Visually impaired persons are in the specially protected category — you must yield.

When entering a wider road from a narrower road, you are on the narrow road so you must yield to traffic on the wider road.

○

Vehicles on the wider road have priority — this is one of the basic rules.

When turning right, there is an oncoming motorcycle going straight. Because a motorcycle is small and easy to pass in front of, it is fine to turn first.

✗

A right-turning vehicle must yield to oncoming vehicles going straight, including motorcycles. Failing to notice motorcycles is in fact the most common cause of right-turn accidents.
